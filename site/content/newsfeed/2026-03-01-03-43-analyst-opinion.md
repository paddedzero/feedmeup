## Article 1: Aeternum botnet hides commands in Polygon smart contracts

The Aeternum

### Technical Analysis: What's Really Happening

### The Mechanic: What's Actually Happening

For decades, the "cat and mouse" game of botnet takedowns followed a predictable script. An adversary stood up a Command and Control (C2) server, defenders identified the IP or domain, and then—through a combination of ISP pressure, registrar seizures, or sinkholing—the infrastructure was neutralized. We called it "whack-a-mole," but at least we had a hammer. With the emergence of the **Aeternum botnet**, the moles have moved into a fortress built of immutable code.

Aeternum represents a sophisticated architectural shift: it utilizes **Polygon smart contracts** as its primary C2 mechanism. In a traditional setup, a compromised "bot" beacons out to a specific URL to receive instructions. In Aeternum’s world, the bot queries a public blockchain. Specifically, the malware is hardcoded with a contract address on the Polygon network—a Layer 2 scaling solution for Ethereum. To update instructions, the attacker simply sends a transaction to that contract. The bots, using standard JSON-RPC calls (the language of Web3), "read" the state of the contract to determine their next move—whether that’s launching a DDoS attack, exfiltrating data, or downloading secondary payloads.

We need to be clear: this isn't just "malware using crypto." It is **Infrastructure-as-Code (IaC) for the dark web**. By leveraging Polygon, the attackers gain three massive advantages. First, **censorship resistance**: no government or ISP can "shut down" a smart contract once it is deployed. Second, **low overhead**: Polygon’s gas fees are negligible, allowing the attacker to update commands for pennies. Third, **stealth through legitimacy**: the traffic generated by the bots often mirrors legitimate Web3 activity, blending into the background noise of decentralized finance (DeFi) and NFT applications.

The technical brilliance—and the danger—lies in the **abstraction of the C2**. The attacker no longer owns a server; they own a "state" on a global, distributed ledger. When a bot queries an RPC (Remote Procedure Call) node like Infura or Alchemy to check the contract, it isn't talking to the attacker. It’s talking to a multi-billion dollar infrastructure provider. Blocking these providers is the digital equivalent of burning down a post office because a criminal sent a letter through it.

---

### The "So What?": Why This Matters

This isn't a curiosity; it is a **structural failure of the current defensive perimeter**. Most modern security stacks rely on "Known Bad" reputation feeds. We block malicious IPs, we flag suspicious domains, and we monitor for DGA (Domain Generation Algorithms). Aeternum bypasses this entire paradigm. 

**The "Unstoppable" Persistence:**
Traditional takedowns are physical or administrative. You seize a server in a data center or you convince a registrar to revoke a domain. You cannot "seize" a smart contract. Even if the original developer’s wallet is blacklisted by an exchange, the contract remains live on the Polygon mainnet, executing its logic for anyone who queries it. This grants the Aeternum botnet a level of **operational longevity** we haven't seen since the early days of peer-to-peer (P2P) botnets like Zeus, but without the complexity of maintaining a P2P mesh.

**The Signal-to-Noise Crisis:**
As enterprises embrace Web3—whether for supply chain tracking, digital identity, or treasury management—the volume of traffic to blockchain RPC nodes is skyrocketing. Security Operations Centers (SOCs) are already drowning in alerts. Aeternum’s traffic doesn't look like a "call home" to a Russian bulletproof host; it looks like a routine query to a legitimate API. This **lowers the barrier to entry** for mid-tier threat actors to maintain persistent, un-killable backdoors into high-value networks.

**The Economic Asymmetry:**
The cost for an attacker to pivot their infrastructure is now virtually zero. If one smart contract is somehow "flagged" (though still functional), they can deploy a new one for less than a dollar. Meanwhile, the cost for an enterprise to hunt for these "silent" queries across thousands of endpoints is astronomical. We are seeing the birth of **"Serverless Malware,"** where the attacker offloads the entire burden of uptime and availability to the blockchain itself.

---

### Strategic Defense: What To Do About It

Defending against Aeternum requires moving away from destination-based filtering and toward **behavioral intent and protocol-level scrutiny**. If you are looking for "bad" IPs, you have already lost.

#### 1. Immediate Actions (Tactical Response)

*   **Audit and Restrict RPC Traffic:** Most corporate workstations have zero legitimate reason to communicate with blockchain RPC endpoints (e.g., `polygon-rpc.com`, `infura.io`, `alchemy.com`). Use your EDR or Web Gateway to **log and alert on any outbound JSON-RPC calls** originating from non-developer or non-finance segments.
*   **Implement TLS Inspection for API Calls:** Since these bots use HTTPS to communicate with RPC nodes, encrypted traffic will hide the payload. You must intercept and inspect the **POST body** of these requests. Look for specific JSON methods like `eth_call` or `eth_getStorageAt` directed toward the same contract address across multiple endpoints—this is a definitive "heartbeat" of a blockchain-based botnet.
*   **DNS Sinkholing of Public Nodes:** If your organization does not participate in the Web3 ecosystem, sinkhole the DNS for known public RPC gateways. This forces the malware to use its own (likely more detectable) infrastructure or fail to receive commands.

#### 2. Long-Term Strategy (The Pivot)

*   **Shift to "Default Deny" Egress:** The era of "allow all outbound" is over. Security Architects must move toward a model where **egress traffic is restricted by application identity**, not just port or protocol. A PDF reader or a calculator app should never be initiating a connection to a blockchain gateway. 
*   **Behavioral Baselining of "The Beacon":** Aeternum bots must check the contract state at regular intervals. Focus your threat hunting on **temporal patterns**. Use your SIEM to identify "long-tail" connections—low-frequency, high-consistency queries to legitimate API providers. The destination is "clean," but the pattern is "malicious."
*   **Zero Trust at the API Layer:** Treat public blockchain gateways as untrusted third-party APIs. Just as you would scrutinize traffic to GitHub or Pastebin (common sites for hosting malicious code), you must apply the same **reputational and behavioral logic** to blockchain interactions. If an endpoint is querying a smart contract that has no known business purpose, isolate it immediately.

**Final Verdict:** Aeternum is a warning shot. It proves that the "decentralized web" is not just a playground for speculators, but a robust, high-availability hosting provider for the next generation of malware. If your defense strategy still relies on the idea that you can "turn off" the attacker's server, you are defending against a threat model that no longer exists.

### Technical Analysis: What's Really Happening

### The Mechanic: What's Actually Happening

When we talk about the Lazarus Group, we are no longer talking about a group of basement-dwelling hackers or even a traditional military intelligence unit. We are looking at a state-sponsored conglomerate that operates with the efficiency of a Fortune 500 company and the ruthlessness of a cartel. Their recent pivot to **Medusa Ransomware**—bolstered by a supporting cast of the **Comebacker** backdoor, the **Blindingcan** RAT, and the **Infohook** stealer—represents a sophisticated evolution in North Korea’s quest for hard currency.

The technical reality here isn't just about a new strain of file-locking code; it’s about the **orchestration of the pipeline**. In this campaign, we see a distinct multi-stage approach that bypasses traditional signature-based defenses. It begins with the **Comebacker** backdoor, which serves as the "scout." It’s lightweight, designed to slip past initial perimeter checks and establish a quiet, persistent foothold. Once the environment is deemed "high value," the heavy machinery moves in. **Blindingcan**, a Remote Access Trojan (RAT) we’ve seen Lazarus refine over years, provides the attackers with full administrative control. This isn't a smash-and-grab; it’s an occupation. They spend days, sometimes weeks, mapping the network, identifying the crown jewels, and—crucially—disabling backups.

The introduction of **Infohook** into this chain is a calculated move toward "triple extortion." Before a single file is encrypted by Medusa, Infohook is used to exfiltrate sensitive data. This ensures that even if a victim has immutable backups and can restore their systems, Lazarus still holds the "leak" card over their heads. Only after the data is safely on a North Korean server do they deploy **Medusa**. By opting for Medusa—a well-known Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS) tool—Lazarus is outsourcing the "final mile" of the attack. They don't need to maintain their own encryption binaries when they can leverage a proven, high-performance engine that is already optimized to evade common EDR (Endpoint Detection and Response) hooks.

We are witnessing the **industrialization of the attack chain**. Lazarus is no longer building every tool from scratch. They are acting as a general contractor, using their custom-built "scaffolding" (Comebacker and Blindingcan) to support the deployment of "off-the-shelf" commercial-grade malware (Medusa). This hybrid model allows them to move faster, scale their operations, and—most importantly—obfuscate their fingerprints.

### The "So What?": Why This Matters

For the C-suite, the "So What?" is simple but devastating: **The line between "Cybercrime" and "State-Sponsored Espionage" has officially evaporated.** 

Historically, CISOs could categorize threats. You had the "noisy" ransomware gangs who wanted a quick payout, and you had the "silent" APTs who wanted your IP. Your defense strategy for one was often different from the other. Lazarus has broken that model. By using Medusa, a tool associated with the broader e-crime ecosystem, they are effectively "hiding in the noise." When your IR team sees Medusa, their playbook might point toward a standard RaaS recovery. They might not realize they are actually tangling with a state-level adversary that has already planted deep-seated persistence via Blindingcan in parts of the network the ransomware didn't even touch.

This shift lowers the barrier to entry for the attacker while exponentially raising the stakes for the defender. Because Lazarus is leveraging the RaaS infrastructure, they benefit from the rapid development cycles of the criminal underground. If a security vendor releases a patch that breaks a specific version of Medusa, the RaaS operators fix it within hours. Lazarus gets that "update" for free, allowing them to focus their elite engineering talent on the more difficult tasks of initial entry and long-term persistence.

Furthermore, this creates a massive **Insurance and Legal Headache**. Most cyber insurance policies have "Act of War" or "State Actor" exclusions. If a company is hit by Medusa, is it a criminal act (covered) or a state-sponsored attack by North Korea (potentially excluded)? By using Medusa, Lazarus creates enough "reasonable doubt" to complicate the recovery and legal process for the victim, adding a layer of psychological and financial pressure that few organizations are prepared to handle. 

This isn't just about data loss; it’s about a **Total Systemic Failure**. When an actor as persistent as Lazarus gains the ability to deploy ransomware at scale, they aren't just looking for a $5 million payout. They are testing the resilience of Western financial and critical infrastructure. They are learning how we respond, how our insurance markets react, and how quickly we can (or can't) recover.

### Strategic Defense: What To Do About It

Defending against a hybrid threat like Lazarus/Medusa requires a bifurcated strategy. You cannot "firewall" your way out of this. You must assume the "scout" (Comebacker) is already looking for a way in.

#### 1. Immediate Actions (Tactical Response)

*   **Aggressive Egress Filtering:** Lazarus relies heavily on custom C2 (Command and Control) protocols for Blindingcan and Comebacker. Implement strict egress filtering that blocks all outbound traffic from servers and workstations except to known-good, required destinations. If a server in your DMZ starts talking to an unknown IP in Southeast Asia over a non-standard port, that should be an automated "kill" trigger, not just an alert.
*   **Credential Guard and LSASS Protection:** Lazarus loves to harvest credentials once they have a foothold. Enable **Windows Defender Credential Guard** and ensure **LSASS (Local Security Authority Subsystem Service)** protection is active to prevent memory dumping. This slows down the lateral movement phase, forcing the attacker to use noisier, more detectable methods.
*   **Audit "Living off the Land" (LotL) Binaries:** Medusa and its precursors often use legitimate system tools (like PowerShell, WMI, and BCP) to move through the network. Implement **AppLocker** or **Windows Defender Application Control (WDAC)** in "Constrained Language Mode" for PowerShell and monitor for unusual parent-child process relationships (e.g., a web server spawning `cmd.exe` or `powershell.exe`).

#### 2. Long-Term Strategy (The Pivot)

*   **The "Blast Radius" Architecture:** Move away from a flat network. If Lazarus deploys Medusa, it should only be able to see a small fraction of your environment. This requires **Micro-segmentation** at the identity and network levels. Use **Zero Trust Architecture (ZTA)** principles where every access request—even inside the perimeter—is verified. If the "Finance" segment is hit, the "Engineering" segment should remain untouchable.
*   **Immutable, Air-Gapped Recovery:** Since Lazarus specifically targets backups before triggering Medusa, your backup strategy must be "out of band." This means **Immutable Backups** (S3 Object Lock or similar) and at least one "Air-Gapped" copy that is physically or logically disconnected from the primary network. Test your restoration speed; if it takes you three weeks to restore from air-gapped tape, Lazarus has already won the psychological war.
*   **Adversarial Threat Hunting:** Stop waiting for your EDR to turn red. Commission or build a **Threat Hunting** capability that specifically looks for the "low and slow" indicators of Lazarus. This means searching for the specific obfuscation patterns used in **Comebacker** and the unique registry keys associated with **Blindingcan**. You need to find them during the "occupation" phase, before the "demolition" (Medusa) begins.

In the end, the Lazarus Group’s adoption of Medusa is a reminder that in the digital age, **sovereign power and criminal enterprise are two sides of the same coin.** They are playing a long game. To survive, your security posture must be equally persistent, equally adaptive, and entirely unsentimental.

## Article 3: ManoMano data breach: massive DIY chain incident impacts 38 million customers - here's what we know

French ecommerce site suffers third-party breach, losing data on millions of customers.

<a href="https://www.techradar.com/pro/security/manomano-data-breach-massive-diy-chain-incident-impacts-38-million-customers-heres-what-we-know" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="inline-flex items-center justify-center rounded-md text-sm font-bold tracking-wide transition-colors bg-primary !text-primary-foreground hover:bg-primary/90 hover:!text-primary-foreground h-9 px-4 py-2 no-underline shadow-sm mt-4">Read Full Article →</a>

### Technical Analysis: What's Really Happening

### The Mechanic: What’s Actually Happening

When a company like ManoMano—a titan of the European DIY and gardening e-commerce space—loses control of 38 million customer records, the post-mortem usually follows a tired, predictable script. The PR team points toward a "third-party partner," the lawyers begin drafting the "we take your privacy seriously" emails, and the security community sighs at yet another massive data leak. But if we peel back the corporate veneer, the ManoMano incident isn't just another breach; it is a clinical demonstration of the **Data Gravity Trap**. 

The technical reality here isn't likely a sophisticated zero-day exploit against ManoMano’s core infrastructure. Instead, we are looking at a failure of **peripheral visibility**. In the modern e-commerce stack, data doesn't sit in a single, fortified vault. It flows. It is synced to marketing automation platforms, pushed to logistics aggregators, and mirrored into "data lakes" for the sake of business intelligence. Based on the scale of this incident—38 million records—the breach almost certainly occurred at a point of **data aggregation**. Whether it was a misconfigured Snowflake instance, an exposed S3 bucket belonging to a sub-processor, or a compromised API key used by a third-party analytics firm, the result is the same: ManoMano’s "DIY" ethos failed to extend to their own supply chain security.

I’ve seen this pattern repeat across the 2026 landscape. We’ve moved past the era of hackers "breaking in" through the front door. Today, they simply find the most over-privileged service account in a secondary environment. The attack chain likely involved **identity-based lateral movement**. An attacker gains access to a third-party vendor’s environment, finds a hardcoded credential or an orphaned session token, and uses it to query a production-adjacent database. Because the traffic comes from a "trusted" partner, the internal SOC alerts are either silenced or never triggered. This isn't a failure of firewalls; it’s a failure of **authorization logic** at the data layer.

### The "So What?": Why This Matters

If you are a CISO sitting on a similar pile of customer data, the ManoMano breach should be a wake-up call that your **attack surface is no longer defined by your IP addresses.** It is defined by your contracts. The "So What?" here is twofold: the weaponization of "intent data" and the total collapse of the traditional perimeter.

First, let’s talk about the data itself. In the DIY sector, purchase history is incredibly revealing. Knowing that a customer just bought 500 square feet of flooring, a specific brand of power saw, and a smart home security kit provides a blueprint for **hyper-personalized social engineering**. In 2026, attackers aren't sending generic "Your account is locked" emails. They are using LLM-driven agents to craft "Your [Specific Brand] Circular Saw has a critical safety recall—click here to schedule a free replacement" messages. By losing 38 million records, ManoMano hasn't just lost emails; they’ve handed a goldmine of **contextual ammunition** to every phishing syndicate in the world.

Second, this breach highlights the **regulatory impotence** of current frameworks. Despite years of GDPR enforcement, we are seeing the "Third-Party Loophole" being exploited at scale. Companies are outsourcing their data processing to minimize operational overhead, but they are inadvertently outsourcing their risk to entities with a fraction of their security budget. This incident lowers the barrier to entry for mid-tier threat actors. They no longer need to bypass ManoMano’s Tier-1 defenses; they just need to find the one "Growth Hacking" startup ManoMano shared their database with three years ago. This is a **systemic risk** to the e-commerce model: the more you personalize, the more you expose.

### Strategic Defense: What To Do About It

The solution isn't more firewalls. It’s a fundamental shift in how we treat data as it leaves our immediate control. We need to move from "Trust but Verify" to a model of **Cryptographic Accountability.**

#### 1. Immediate Actions (Tactical Response)

*   **Kill-Switch Third-Party API Integrations:** Conduct an immediate audit of all active API keys and service accounts. Any integration that hasn't seen traffic in 30 days should be disabled. For active ones, implement **IP allow-listing** and **request-rate limiting** specifically for data-heavy queries.
*   **Enforce Tokenization at the Edge:** Stop sending raw PII (Personally Identifiable Information) to third parties. Use a **Data Privacy Vault** (like Skyflow or VGS) to tokenize sensitive fields. If your marketing partner needs to send an email, they should receive a token, not the actual email address. This ensures that if they are breached, the data is useless to the attacker.
*   **Credential Rotation & Secret Management:** If you haven't rotated your cloud provider secrets or database credentials in the last 90 days, do it now. Use tools like **HashiCorp Vault** or **AWS Secrets Manager** to move toward dynamic, short-lived credentials rather than static keys that can live forever in a developer’s `.env` file.

#### 2. Long-Term Strategy (The Pivot)

*   **Implement Data Security Posture Management (DSPM):** Most CISOs can tell you where their servers are, but few can tell you where their data is. Deploy a DSPM solution (e.g., **Sentra** or **Cyera**) to automatically discover "shadow data"—copies of your production database that developers have spun up in dev/test environments or shared with unauthorized SaaS apps. You cannot protect what you don't know exists.
*   **Zero Trust Data Architecture (ZTDA):** Shift the focus from network-centric security to data-centric security. This involves using **Attribute-Based Access Control (ABAC)**. Instead of saying "Marketing can access the database," your policy should be: "Marketing can only access 'Email' and 'Name' fields, only during business hours, and only if the request originates from a managed device with a valid MFA session."
*   **Supply Chain "Right to Audit" with Teeth:** Move beyond the "Security Questionnaire" (which everyone lies on). Your contracts must include the right to perform **automated security assessments** or require the vendor to provide real-time visibility into their security posture via an API. If they can't prove their encryption-at-rest and logging standards, they don't get the data.

The ManoMano breach is a reminder that in the DIY world, if you don't build your own security foundation, the whole house eventually comes down. **Stop treating third-party risk as a legal checkbox and start treating it as a technical vulnerability.**
